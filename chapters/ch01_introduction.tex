\chapter{Introduction and Motivation}
\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Light and the Materials That Shape It}

Light is one of the most carefully studied phenomena in all of science, yet
its interaction with matter continues to produce surprises. When a beam of
light enters a material, the electrons in that material respond to the
oscillating electric field of the light wave. This response determines
everything we observe: how much light is reflected, how much passes through,
how the direction of propagation changes, and how quickly the energy is
absorbed. For centuries, the materials available to scientists were the ones
found in nature, and their optical properties were fixed by chemistry and
crystal structure.

Over the past three decades, a different idea has emerged. Instead of
accepting the materials that nature provides, researchers began asking whether
it is possible to \emph{engineer} optical properties by structuring matter at
scales smaller than the wavelength of light. If a composite structure is built
from two different materials arranged in layers or patterns whose feature size
is far below one wavelength, then the light cannot resolve the individual
components. The wave sees only the average response of the composite. By
choosing the right materials and the right geometry, one can in principle
design a response that no naturally occurring material possesses. Such
engineered composites are called \textbf{metamaterials}, from the Greek prefix
\emph{meta}, meaning beyond.

Metamaterials have produced several striking results. Materials with a
negative refractive index were demonstrated experimentally in the microwave
regime in 2001, confirming a theoretical prediction from the 1960s that had
long been considered a mathematical curiosity. Perfect lensing, invisibility
cloaking at specific frequencies, and extreme light confinement have all been
demonstrated in various forms. The field has matured from a proof of concept
into a rich area of practical photonics engineering.

This report concerns a particular class of metamaterial known as a
\textbf{hyperbolic metamaterial}, and specifically a thin multilayer stack
composed of nickel (Ni) and titanium dioxide (TiO$_2$). The central goal is to
determine, from optical measurements, how light actually propagates inside
this structure at visible and near-infrared wavelengths.

\section{Hyperbolic Metamaterials: A New Kind of Optical Anisotropy}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

To understand what makes a hyperbolic metamaterial special, it helps to first
recall how light propagates in an ordinary anisotropic crystal such as calcite
or quartz. In such a crystal, the speed of light depends on the direction of
propagation and on the polarisation of the wave. This property, called
birefringence, arises because the electronic structure of the crystal is not
the same in every direction. The crystal has a preferred axis called the optic
axis, and the response of the electrons to light is different along this axis
compared to the directions perpendicular to it.

A compact way to describe this directional response is through the
\textbf{permittivity tensor} $\bar{\bar{\varepsilon}}$. The permittivity is
the quantity that tells us how strongly the electrons in a material respond to
an applied electric field. In an isotropic material, this response is the same
in every direction, and the permittivity is a single number. In a uniaxial
anisotropic material, the permittivity along the optic axis, which we call
$\eperp$, differs from the permittivity in the plane perpendicular to that
axis, which we call $\epar$. The tensor is diagonal,
\begin{equation}
  \bar{\bar{\varepsilon}} = \begin{pmatrix}
    \epar & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \epar & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \eperp
  \end{pmatrix},
  \label{eq:ch1_tensor}
\end{equation}
where we have placed the optic axis along the $z$ direction. For a layered
stack, $z$ is the direction normal to the layers, so $\eperp$ describes the
response to fields oriented perpendicular to the layer planes, and $\epar$
describes the response to fields oscillating within the layer planes.

In a natural crystal, both $\epar$ and $\eperp$ are positive real numbers.
The crystal is a dielectric in both directions, meaning that light propagating
in any direction encounters a material that supports oscillating waves without
rapidly absorbing them. The surface in wavevector space that describes all the
directions a wave can travel at a fixed frequency is a closed surface: an
ellipsoid. An ellipsoid is a stretched sphere, and it curves back on itself
in every direction. No matter how the wave is oriented, it always remains
within the same bounded region of wavevector space.

A hyperbolic metamaterial does something fundamentally different. By mixing a
metal and a dielectric in the right proportion and geometry, it becomes
possible to make $\epar$ and $\eperp$ have \emph{opposite signs}. One
component of the permittivity tensor is negative, like a metal, while the
other is positive, like a dielectric. This sign difference has a dramatic
consequence for wave propagation.

The surface in wavevector space that describes the allowed propagation
directions is no longer a closed ellipsoid. It opens up into a hyperboloid: a
surface that extends outward to arbitrarily large wavevectors. This is the
defining property of a hyperbolic medium, and it is the origin of the name.
Depending on which permittivity component is negative, the two qualitatively
different open topologies are called Type-I and Type-II, as illustrated in
Figure~\ref{fig:ch1_ifc_regimes}.

The practical importance of this open topology is substantial. Modes with very
large wavevectors carry spatial information at very fine scales. In a normal
dielectric or even in a conventional high-index material, these short-wavelength
modes are evanescent: they decay exponentially away from any source that
excites them and cannot propagate. In a hyperbolic medium, they can propagate
freely into the bulk. This means that a hyperbolic medium can carry information
at spatial scales far below what conventional optics allows, which has direct
applications in subdiffraction imaging, enhanced spontaneous emission, and
nanoscale heat transfer between objects.

\section{The Ni/TiO\texorpdfstring{$_2$}{2} Multilayer System}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

There are many ways to build a hyperbolic metamaterial. One of the most
practical approaches at visible and near-infrared wavelengths is to stack thin
alternating layers of a metal and a dielectric, with each layer far thinner
than the wavelength of light. When the individual layers are thin enough, the
electromagnetic wave cannot resolve them individually. The stack behaves as if
it were a single homogeneous anisotropic medium, with effective permittivities
determined by the properties of both constituent materials and by the fraction
of the total thickness that is occupied by the metal.

The metal used here is \textbf{nickel} (Ni). Nickel has strong metallic
character at visible wavelengths: its real permittivity is large and negative
across most of the visible spectrum, meaning that it reflects rather than
transmits light. At the same time, nickel is compatible with standard physical
vapour deposition and adheres well to oxide surfaces.

The dielectric used here is \textbf{titanium dioxide} (TiO$_2$). Titanium
dioxide is a high-index transparent oxide with a refractive index of
approximately 2.4 at $650$\,nm. It is chemically stable, mechanically robust,
and well-suited to thin film deposition. The combination of a high-index
dielectric with a metallic component is a recipe for achieving sign changes in
the effective permittivity at manageable metal fill fractions.

The sample studied in this work consists of three repeating periods of
TiO$_2$ and Ni, deposited by physical vapour deposition on a borosilicate
glass substrate. The layer order, viewed from the air side downward, is
\begin{equation}
  \text{air} \;\Big|\;
  \underbrace{\text{TiO}_2 \;\Big|\; \text{Ni}}_{\times\,3} \;\Big|\;
  \text{SiO}_2 \;\text{(substrate)},
  \label{eq:ch1_stack}
\end{equation}
with TiO$_2$ facing the air and Ni deposited first on the glass. The
individual layer thicknesses were measured by focused ion beam cross-sectional
transmission electron microscopy (FIB/TEM) and are listed in
Table~\ref{tab:ch1_thicknesses}. The total stack thickness is approximately
$44.3$\,nm, which is far below the shortest wavelength studied ($550$\,nm).
This confirms that the stack is well within the subwavelength regime where the
effective medium description is valid.

\section{The Central Scientific Question}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Given the structure described above, the natural question is: \emph{what does
the isofrequency contour of this stack actually look like, and is it genuinely
hyperbolic?}

From a purely theoretical perspective, one might think this question has a
simple answer. If we know the optical constants of bulk Ni and bulk TiO$_2$,
and if we know the layer thicknesses and fill fraction, we can use the
effective medium theory (derived in Chapter~\ref{ch:emt}) to compute the
effective permittivities $\epar$ and $\eperp$ and then plot the IFC directly.

This approach gives a useful starting point. Using the Ni optical constants
from Johnson and Christy (1974)~\cite{johnson_christy} and the TiO$_2$
constants from Jolivet et al.\ (2023)~\cite{jolivet} for amorphous ALD
TiO$_2$, the effective medium theory predicts that $\Re(\epar)$ crosses zero
near $607$\,nm, placing the stack nominally in the Type-II hyperbolic regime
at longer wavelengths. However, there are several reasons why this prediction
cannot be trusted without experimental verification.

The first reason is that the Ni films in this stack are only $5$ to $6$\,nm
thick. At this scale, the optical properties of a deposited metal film can
differ substantially from those of the bulk material. Electrons in a thin film
scatter from the surfaces and grain boundaries far more frequently than in a
bulk sample, and this additional scattering changes the optical constants. In
addition, nickel oxidises rapidly in air, forming a thin NiO layer at each
surface. NiO is an insulator with very different optical properties from
metallic Ni. Both of these effects tend to make the deposited films less
metallic than the bulk prediction suggests.

The second reason is that the effective medium theory itself is an
approximation. It assumes that the layer interfaces are perfectly sharp and
that there is no interdiffusion between the Ni and TiO$_2$. In a real
sputtered film deposited at room temperature, some degree of interfacial
roughness and atomic mixing is unavoidable. These effects alter the effective
response of the stack in ways that are difficult to predict without a
measurement.

The third and most physically interesting reason is the role of loss. Even if
the effective medium prediction were perfectly accurate, the Ni layers carry a
very large imaginary part of the permittivity, meaning they absorb light
strongly. In Chapter~\ref{ch:em_theory} we will show rigorously that loss
changes the topology of the IFC. What appears as an open hyperbola in a
lossless calculation becomes a closed, loss-broadened contour in reality.
Whether the IFC is truly open or merely appears open depends on quantitative
details, not just the sign of the real permittivity.

For all of these reasons, the shape of the IFC must be determined from a
measurement of the actual fabricated sample rather than inferred from bulk
databases alone. This measurement-based determination of the IFC is the
experimental and computational challenge that drives the entire methodology
developed in this report.

\section{Why Simple Intensity Measurements Are Not Enough}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The most straightforward way to measure the optical response of a thin film is
to shine light on it and record how much is reflected and how much is
transmitted. If we do this at many angles of incidence and over a range of
wavelengths, we obtain $R(\theta, \lambda)$ and $T(\theta, \lambda)$: the
angle-resolved and spectrally resolved reflectance and transmittance. From
these data, one might hope to extract both $\epar$ and $\eperp$ by fitting a
model to the measurements.

In principle this is correct: the transfer matrix method, which we derive in
Chapter~\ref{ch:tmm_derivation}, predicts exactly what $R$ and $T$ should be
for any given set of material parameters. One can in principle search for the
parameters that best reproduce the measured data.

In practice, a fundamental problem arises. The out-of-plane permittivity
$\eperp$ enters the reflectance and transmittance of a subwavelength film only
through a very small correction term. We derive this explicitly in
Chapter~\ref{ch:why_rt_failed}. For a $44$\,nm film, this correction
contributes roughly $2\%$ of the total signal at the largest accessible angles
of incidence, which falls below the level of experimental noise in any
realistic measurement. In a statistical analysis, the uncertainty on the
extracted $\eperp$ from $R$ and $T$ alone turns out to be orders of magnitude
larger than the value itself. The measurement simply does not contain enough
information to determine $\eperp$.

The physical reason for this insensitivity is not a technical limitation that
better instruments could overcome. It is a fundamental consequence of the
thinness of the film. The out-of-plane permittivity $\eperp$ controls how the
out-of-plane component of the electric field, $E_z$, evolves as the wave
travels through the stack. But in a $44$\,nm film at $700$\,nm wavelength, the
optical phase accumulated across the stack in the $z$ direction is small:
$k_0 d \approx 0.40$ radians. The wave traverses the entire film before the
$\eperp$-dependent phase has a chance to grow to a detectable level.

This is why the work described in this report uses \textbf{spectroscopic
ellipsometry} as the primary measurement technique.

Ellipsometry measures not just the intensity of the reflected light, but the
\textbf{change in polarisation state} that occurs upon reflection. Specifically,
it records the complex ratio of the reflection coefficients for $p$-polarised
and $s$-polarised light. This ratio carries both an amplitude component and a
phase component. The phase component is denoted $\Delta$, and it is
particularly sensitive to optical path length differences between the two
polarisations inside the stack. Crucially, the $p$-polarised wave has an
out-of-plane electric field component $E_z$ that the $s$-polarised wave does
not. The ellipsometric phase $\Delta$ therefore probes the out-of-plane
response in a way that the intensities $R$ and $T$ cannot.

We show in Chapter~\ref{ch:why_rt_failed} that the deep minimum in $\Delta$
near the pseudo-Brewster angle, which occurs around $75^\circ$ angle of
incidence for this sample, provides a detectable and identifiable signature of
$\eperp$ even in a $44$\,nm film. This is the key measurement that makes the
tensor extraction possible.

\section{Road Map of This Report}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The remainder of this report builds the complete picture from first principles,
following a logical chain from Maxwell's equations through to the final
reconstructed IFC.

\textbf{Chapter~\ref{ch:em_theory}} derives the electromagnetic theory of
wave propagation in a uniaxial medium, starting from Maxwell's curl equations.
We obtain the TM dispersion relation that gives the IFC equation, explain why
hyperbolicity is a TM-only phenomenon, and work through the full lossy IFC
derivation following Jackson et al.\ (2024)~\cite{jackson2024}, showing
analytically why material loss closes the open hyperboloid into a bounded
surface with a finite maximum wavevector.

\textbf{Chapter~\ref{ch:emt}} derives the effective medium theory for a
metal-dielectric multilayer from the electromagnetic boundary conditions. We
obtain the arithmetic mean formula for $\epar$ and the harmonic mean formula
for $\eperp$, and explain why the two components average differently. We apply
the theory numerically to the Ni/TiO$_2$ system and discuss its limitations
honestly.

\textbf{Chapter~\ref{ch:sample_measurements}} describes the sample in detail
and presents both sets of experimental measurements used in this work:
angle-resolved reflectance and transmittance data, and the spectroscopic
ellipsometry data collected on a J.A.\ Woollam M-2000 variable angle
spectroscopic ellipsometer at eight angles of incidence from $45^\circ$ to
$80^\circ$.

\textbf{Chapter~\ref{ch:tmm_derivation}} derives the transfer matrix method
step by step, beginning from the boundary conditions at a single planar
interface. We build the formalism layer by layer until we have a complete model
for the full six-layer stack using the exact FIB/TEM thicknesses. We extend
the standard isotropic TMM to handle uniaxial anisotropic layers, and we
derive the ellipsometric observables $\Psi$, $\Delta$, and the Stokes-like
parameters $N$, $C$, $S$ from the complex reflection amplitudes.

\textbf{Chapter~\ref{ch:forward_validation}} validates the forward model by
comparing its predictions to the measured $R$, $T$, and ellipsometric data.
We discuss the systematic discrepancy that appears when bulk Ni constants are
used, and identify its physical origins in thin-film electron scattering and
surface oxidation.

\textbf{Chapter~\ref{ch:why_rt_failed}} explains in full detail why the
reflectance and transmittance data alone are insufficient to recover $\eperp$,
and why ellipsometry changes the situation. This chapter contains the
thin-film expansion argument, a Monte Carlo sensitivity analysis, and a
discussion of the pseudo-Brewster angle signature that makes the ellipsometric
extraction of $\eperp$ possible.

\textbf{Chapter~\ref{ch:inverse_methodology}} describes the inverse extraction
procedure: how we set up the optimisation problem, which parameters are fixed
and which are free, how the cost function is constructed in the $N$, $C$, $S$
space, and how the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm finds the best-fit tensor at
each wavelength.

\textbf{Chapter~\ref{ch:results}} presents the extracted permittivity spectra
$\epar(\lambda)$ and $\eperp(\lambda)$ with uncertainty estimates, compares
them to the EMT prediction, and uses the Jackson et al.\ (2024) lossy
complex-wavevector method to reconstruct the final isofrequency contour.

\textbf{Chapter~\ref{ch:limitations}} discusses the limitations of the present
approach and outlines the next experimental steps, in particular the total
internal reflection ellipsometry experiment using a ZnSe prism coupler to
access wavevectors beyond the air light line.

Throughout this report we use SI units and the time-harmonic convention
$e^{-\ii\omega t}$. All permittivities are complex, written as
$\eps = \eps_1 + \ii\,\eps_2$ with $\eps_2 \geq 0$ for a passive absorbing
material. The in-plane wavevector is written $k_\parallel$ or $k_x$
interchangeably. The wavevector component along the optic axis is $k_z$ or
$k_\perp$.

\begin{resultbox}
\textbf{One-sentence summary of this report.}
We measure the polarisation state of light reflected from a $44$\,nm
Ni/TiO$_2$ multilayer at eight angles of incidence across $450$ to $1000$\,nm,
fit an anisotropic transfer matrix model to extract the permittivity tensor
$(\epar(\lambda),\,\eperp(\lambda))$, and reconstruct the isofrequency
contour, finding a closed loss-broadened ellipse with no open hyperbolic
behaviour across the measured spectral window.
\end{resultbox}
