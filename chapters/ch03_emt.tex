\chapter{Effective Medium Theory for Ni/TiO$_2$}
\label{ch:emt}

The six-layer Ni/TiO$_2$ stack described in Chapter~\ref{ch:sample_measurements}
has a total thickness of only $44.3$\,nm. This is roughly sixteen times smaller
than the shortest wavelength we study ($550$\,nm). When an electromagnetic wave
encounters a structure that is much smaller than its wavelength, it cannot
resolve the individual layers. The wave responds instead to an average of the
material properties, as if the entire stack were replaced by a single slab of
some homogeneous material. Effective medium theory (EMT) is the framework that
tells us what that average material looks like. This chapter derives the EMT
formulas from first principles and applies them to the Ni/TiO$_2$ system.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{The Sub-Wavelength Limit and the Effective Medium Idea}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

To understand why EMT works, consider what happens as light travels through the
multilayer stack. At each interface between Ni and TiO$_2$, part of the wave is
reflected and part is transmitted. These partial waves recombine coherently
inside the stack, interfering with each other. The resulting field distribution
depends on the layer thicknesses and on the optical constants of both materials.

When the layer thicknesses are much smaller than the wavelength, the phase
accumulated across a single layer becomes very small. For one TiO$_2$ layer of
thickness $d \approx 9$\,nm at $\lambda = 700$\,nm:
\begin{equation}
  \delta = k_0\,n\,d \approx \frac{2\pi}{700}\times 2.4\times 9
  \approx 0.19\;\text{rad},
  \label{eq:ch3_phase_per_layer}
\end{equation}
which is roughly $11^\circ$. This is small enough that the wave barely changes
as it traverses one layer, and the details of the layer structure become
irrelevant. What the wave experiences is effectively a homogeneous medium with
some averaged properties.

This average is not a simple arithmetic mean of the two permittivities weighted
by their volume fractions. The correct average depends on the \emph{direction}
in which the electric or displacement field acts relative to the layer planes.
This directional dependence is the key result of EMT, and it comes directly from
the electromagnetic boundary conditions at each interface.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Electromagnetic Boundary Conditions at a Planar Interface}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Before deriving the EMT formulas, we need to recall the two boundary conditions
that the electric and displacement fields must satisfy at every interface between
two media with permittivities $\eps_1$ and $\eps_2$.

The first condition, derived from the integral form of Faraday's law, states
that the \textbf{tangential component of $\Evec$} is continuous:
\begin{equation}
  E_{1,\parallel} = E_{2,\parallel}.
  \label{eq:ch3_E_tangential}
\end{equation}

The second condition, derived from Gauss's law in the absence of free surface
charges, states that the \textbf{normal component of $\Dvec = \eps_0\eps\Evec$}
is continuous:
\begin{equation}
  D_{1,\perp} = D_{2,\perp} \implies
  \eps_1 E_{1,\perp} = \eps_2 E_{2,\perp}.
  \label{eq:ch3_D_normal}
\end{equation}

These two conditions lead to very different results depending on whether the
applied field is tangential or normal to the layer planes, and that asymmetry
is what drives the difference between $\epar$ and $\eperp$.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{In-Plane Permittivity \texorpdfstring{$\epar$}{eps\_par}:
         Capacitors in Parallel}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Consider a macroscopic electric field $E_\parallel$ applied parallel to the
layer planes (along the $x$ or $y$ direction). At every Ni/TiO$_2$ interface,
this field is tangential. Condition~\eqref{eq:ch3_E_tangential} therefore
requires the same tangential field in both materials:
\begin{equation}
  E_\parallel^{(\text{Ni})} = E_\parallel^{(\text{TiO}_2)} = E_\parallel.
  \label{eq:ch3_E_same}
\end{equation}
The two layers experience the same electric field. The macroscopic displacement
field is the volume-weighted average of the displacements in each layer. If Ni
occupies a fraction $\rho$ of the total stack thickness and TiO$_2$ occupies
the remaining fraction $(1-\rho)$, then
\begin{equation}
  D_\parallel = \rho\,\eps_0\eps_\text{Ni}\,E_\parallel
               + (1-\rho)\,\eps_0\eps_\text{TiO_2}\,E_\parallel
              = \eps_0\!\left[\rho\,\eps_\text{Ni}
                + (1-\rho)\,\eps_\text{TiO_2}\right]E_\parallel.
  \label{eq:ch3_D_parallel}
\end{equation}
Comparing with the defining relation $D_\parallel = \eps_0\epar E_\parallel$
gives
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\epar = \rho\,\eps_\text{Ni} + (1-\rho)\,\eps_\text{TiO_2}.}
  \label{eq:ch3_eps_par}
\end{equation}
This is the \textbf{arithmetic mean} (volume average) of the two permittivities,
weighted by their respective volume fractions. Electrically, it is equivalent
to two capacitors connected in parallel: the total capacitance is the sum of
the individual capacitances.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Out-of-Plane Permittivity \texorpdfstring{$\eperp$}{eps\_perp}:
         Capacitors in Series}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Now consider a macroscopic field $E_\perp$ applied perpendicular to the layer
planes (along the $z$ direction). At every interface, this field is normal.
Condition~\eqref{eq:ch3_D_normal} now applies: in the absence of free charges
at the interface, the normal component of $\Dvec$ is continuous:
\begin{equation}
  D_\perp^{(\text{Ni})} = D_\perp^{(\text{TiO}_2)} = D_\perp.
  \label{eq:ch3_D_same}
\end{equation}
The displacement field is the same in both layers, but the electric fields are
different:
\begin{equation}
  E_\perp^{(\text{Ni})} = \frac{D_\perp}{\eps_0\eps_\text{Ni}},
  \qquad
  E_\perp^{(\text{TiO}_2)} = \frac{D_\perp}{\eps_0\eps_\text{TiO_2}}.
  \label{eq:ch3_E_different}
\end{equation}
The macroscopic electric field is the volume-weighted average of the fields in
each layer:
\begin{equation}
  E_\perp = \rho\,E_\perp^{(\text{Ni})} + (1-\rho)\,E_\perp^{(\text{TiO}_2)}
  = \frac{D_\perp}{\eps_0}\!\left[
      \frac{\rho}{\eps_\text{Ni}} + \frac{1-\rho}{\eps_\text{TiO_2}}
    \right].
  \label{eq:ch3_E_average}
\end{equation}
Comparing with $E_\perp = D_\perp/(\eps_0\eperp)$ gives
\begin{equation}
  \frac{1}{\eperp} = \frac{\rho}{\eps_\text{Ni}} + \frac{1-\rho}{\eps_\text{TiO_2}},
  \label{eq:ch3_eps_perp_inv}
\end{equation}
or equivalently
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\eperp = \frac{\eps_\text{Ni}\,\eps_\text{TiO_2}}
                       {\rho\,\eps_\text{TiO_2} + (1-\rho)\,\eps_\text{Ni}}.}
  \label{eq:ch3_eps_perp}
\end{equation}
This is the \textbf{harmonic mean} (reciprocal average) of the two
permittivities. Electrically, it is equivalent to two capacitors in series:
the reciprocal of the total capacitance is the sum of the reciprocals of the
individual capacitances.

Figure~\ref{fig:ch3_bc_cartoon} illustrates the physical origin of the two
different averages. The key point is that the electric field is equalised when
the field is tangential (leading to the arithmetic mean), while the displacement
field is equalised when the field is normal (leading to the harmonic mean). This
asymmetry is built into Maxwell's equations through the boundary conditions and
has nothing to do with approximations or material models.

\section{Why the Two Components Average Differently}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The results of Sections~3.3 and~3.4 reveal a fundamental asymmetry. The
in-plane permittivity $\epar$ is the arithmetic mean of the two constituent
permittivities. The out-of-plane permittivity $\eperp$ is the harmonic mean.
These two averages behave very differently when one constituent is a metal with
$\Re(\eps_\text{Ni}) < 0$.

For the \textbf{arithmetic mean} $\epar$, the metallic and dielectric
contributions compete directly. The Ni contribution, weighted by $\rho = 0.379$,
pulls $\Re(\epar)$ toward negative values, while the TiO$_2$ contribution,
weighted by $1-\rho = 0.621$, pulls it toward positive values. At wavelengths
where the metallic pull wins, $\Re(\epar) < 0$, and the stack behaves as a
metal in the in-plane direction.

For the \textbf{harmonic mean} $\eperp$, the situation is different. The
harmonic mean is dominated by the component with the \emph{smaller} reciprocal,
which is the component with the \emph{larger} absolute permittivity. Since Ni
has $|\eps_\text{Ni}| \gg |\eps_\text{TiO_2}|$ at visible wavelengths, the
$1/\eps_\text{Ni}$ term is much smaller than the $1/\eps_\text{TiO_2}$ term.
The harmonic mean is therefore dominated by the TiO$_2$ response, and
$\Re(\eperp)$ remains close to the TiO$_2$ permittivity throughout the
visible spectrum.

\begin{resultbox}
\textbf{Physical summary.}
$\epar$ is controlled by Ni because the in-plane field experiences both materials
\emph{in parallel}, and the metallic character of Ni pulls the average metallic.
$\eperp$ is controlled by TiO$_2$ because the out-of-plane field experiences
both materials \emph{in series}, and the large $|\eps_\text{Ni}|$ means the Ni
layer acts like a short circuit that leaves the total response insensitive to it.
\end{resultbox}

This asymmetry is precisely what allows the in-plane and out-of-plane
permittivities to have opposite signs in the same structure: $\Re(\epar)$ can
become negative (metallic in-plane character) while $\Re(\eperp)$ remains
positive (dielectric out-of-plane character), which is the definition of a
Type-II hyperbolic metamaterial.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Fill Fraction and Stack Geometry}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The only geometric parameter that appears in the EMT formulas is the metal fill
fraction
\begin{equation}
  \rho = \frac{d_\text{Ni,total}}{d_\text{total}}
        = \frac{d_{\text{Ni},1} + d_{\text{Ni},2} + d_{\text{Ni},3}}
               {d_\text{total}},
  \label{eq:ch3_rho_def}
\end{equation}
where $d_\text{total}$ is the total stack thickness. Using the FIB/TEM-measured
per-layer thicknesses from Table~\ref{tab:ch1_thicknesses}:
\begin{align}
  d_\text{Ni,total} &= 5.556 + 5.556 + 5.688 = 16.800\;\text{nm}, \\
  d_\text{total}    &= 9.171 + 5.556 + 10.361 + 5.556 + 7.981 + 5.688
                      = 44.313\;\text{nm}, \\
  \rho              &= \frac{16.800}{44.313} = 0.379.
  \label{eq:ch3_rho_value}
\end{align}
This fill fraction is the single most important geometric parameter for the
optical properties of the stack. If $\rho$ were much smaller (dilute metal
limit), $\epar$ would be close to $\eps_\text{TiO_2}$ and no hyperbolic
behaviour would occur. If $\rho$ were close to 1 (dense metal limit), both
components would be metallic. The value $\rho = 0.379$ places the stack in
a regime where the arithmetic mean $\epar$ is expected to cross zero near the
middle of the visible spectrum, enabling the hyperbolic window.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Material Optical Constants}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The EMT formulas require the complex permittivities of the two constituent
materials as functions of wavelength. We use two sources.

For \textbf{bulk nickel}, we use the tabulated optical constants of Johnson and
Christy~\cite{johnson_christy}, measured on evaporated Ni films by ellipsometry
at room temperature. These are the most widely cited Ni optical data in the
visible and near-infrared range. The data give complex refractive indices
$n + \ii\kappa$ from which we derive
$\eps_\text{Ni} = (n+\ii\kappa)^2 = (n^2-\kappa^2) + \ii\,2n\kappa$.

For \textbf{amorphous TiO$_2$} deposited by atomic layer deposition (ALD), we
use the optical constants of Jolivet et al.\ (2023)~\cite{jolivet}. Amorphous
ALD TiO$_2$ is optically distinct from crystalline (anatase or rutile) TiO$_2$:
it has a slightly lower refractive index and a smaller band gap, making it
weakly absorbing at wavelengths below about $420$\,nm. The Jolivet data are
the most appropriate choice for our sample because our TiO$_2$ was deposited by
ALD without subsequent annealing.

Several key observations follow from this table.

\textbf{$\epar$: sign change near $607$\,nm.} The real part of the in-plane
permittivity passes through zero at approximately $607$\,nm. This is the
epsilon-near-zero (ENZ) crossing. Below this wavelength, the in-plane response
is dielectric ($\Re(\epar) > 0$). Above it, the in-plane response is metallic
($\Re(\epar) < 0$). The EMT therefore predicts that the stack enters the
nominally Type-II hyperbolic regime at wavelengths above $607$\,nm.

\textbf{$\eperp$: always positive and slowly varying.} The out-of-plane
permittivity is dominated by TiO$_2$ (as argued in Section~3.5) and remains
between $6.5$ and $7.7$ across the entire range. It is weakly wavelength
dependent and never becomes negative. This is consistent with the expectation
for a harmonic-mean response dominated by the high-index dielectric.

\textbf{Large imaginary parts throughout.} The imaginary part of $\epar$ is
between 5 and 8 across the window, while $\Im(\eperp)$ is small but non-zero.
The large $\Im(\epar)$ comes directly from the large $\Im(\eps_\text{Ni})$
weighted by $\rho = 0.379$.

\section{The ENZ Crossing and the Hyperbolic Window}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The wavelength at which $\Re(\epar) = 0$ is called the epsilon-near-zero (ENZ)
point, and it plays a special role in the physics of the stack. At this
wavelength, the arithmetic mean condition $\rho\,\Re(\eps_\text{Ni}) +
(1-\rho)\,\Re(\eps_\text{TiO_2}) = 0$ is satisfied. Solving for $\lambda$:
\begin{equation}
  \Re(\eps_\text{Ni}(\lambda_\text{ENZ}))
  = -\frac{1-\rho}{\rho}\,\Re(\eps_\text{TiO_2}(\lambda_\text{ENZ}))
  \approx -\frac{0.621}{0.379}\times 5.7 \approx -9.3.
  \label{eq:ch3_ENZ_condition}
\end{equation}
From the Johnson and Christy data, Ni reaches $\Re(\eps_\text{Ni}) \approx -9.3$
near $\lambda \approx 430$\,nm. However, when we include the TiO$_2$ wavelength
dependence more carefully, the crossing occurs near $607$\,nm. The reason for
this discrepancy is that equation~\eqref{eq:ch3_ENZ_condition} is wavelength
dependent on both sides, and the crossing must be found self-consistently.

In the lossless picture (imaginary parts set to zero), the wavelength range
where $\Re(\epar) < 0$ and $\Re(\eperp) > 0$ constitutes the \textbf{hyperbolic
window} of the stack. For our Ni/TiO$_2$ system, this window opens at $607$\,nm
according to the EMT prediction and extends to longer wavelengths. Within this
window, the lossless IFC would be a Type-II open hyperbola opening along the
$k_\parallel$ direction, as established in Chapter~\ref{ch:em_theory}.

However, the EMT also predicts a large imaginary part of $\epar$ throughout
the window, which means the hyperbola is heavily damped. Whether any
practically useful hyperbolic propagation exists requires a full lossy
analysis, as performed in Chapter~\ref{ch:em_theory} using the Jackson et al.\
(2024) framework~\cite{jackson2024}.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Loss Tangent Analysis: How Metallic is the Stack?}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

A convenient single-number summary of how ``hyperbolic'' a nominally hyperbolic
medium actually is comes from the \textbf{in-plane loss tangent}:
\begin{equation}
  \tan\delta_\parallel \equiv \frac{\Im(\epar)}{|\Re(\epar)|}.
  \label{eq:ch3_loss_tangent}
\end{equation}
A small loss tangent ($\ll 1$) means the imaginary part is negligible relative
to the real part. In this regime, the lossless hyperbolic picture is a good
approximation: the IFC is open, and modes with large wavevectors propagate with
relatively small attenuation. A loss tangent of order 1 or larger means
imaginary and real parts are comparable: the IFC is significantly broadened and
the maximum wavevector $k_m$ is only modestly larger than $k_0$.

The loss tangent is between 2 and 8 across the entire window. This is two to

$\epar$ dominates the real part at every wavelength. The physical consequence,
derived analytically in Chapter~\ref{ch:em_theory}, is that the IFC is closed
and the maximum wavevector is only modestly above $k_0$.

\section{Why EMT Alone Is Not Sufficient: Thin-Film Effects}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The EMT formulas in equations~\eqref{eq:ch3_eps_par}
and~\eqref{eq:ch3_eps_perp} assume that the optical constants of the
constituent materials are identical to those measured in bulk form. In reality,
the Ni films in our stack are only $5$ to $6$\,nm thick, and the TiO$_2$ films
are between $8$ and $11$\,nm thick. At these thicknesses, several physical
effects push the actual film properties away from the bulk values.

\paragraph{Electron mean free path limitation.}
In bulk Ni, conduction electrons scatter primarily from phonons and grain
boundaries, with a mean free path of roughly $4$\,nm at room temperature. In
a $5$\,nm film, electrons additionally scatter from the top and bottom film
surfaces. This increased scattering rate raises the effective damping in the
Drude response and increases the imaginary part of the permittivity while
reducing the magnitude of its real part. The film therefore becomes less
metallic (smaller $|\Re(\eps)|$) and more lossy (larger $\Im(\eps)$) than the
bulk data would predict.

\paragraph{Nickel oxide formation.}
Nickel oxidises spontaneously in air, forming a thin NiO or Ni(OH)$_2$ layer
at the film surfaces. NiO is a Mott insulator with a band gap of roughly
$3.7$\,eV and a permittivity that is dielectric and positive across the
visible spectrum. Even a $0.5$\,nm surface oxide on each face of a $5.6$\,nm
Ni film represents roughly $18\%$ of the film volume. This replaces metallic
material with insulating material, further reducing the effective metallic
character of the film.

\paragraph{PVD islanding.}
Physical vapour deposition of thin metals often produces films that do not
form a perfectly continuous sheet at very small thicknesses. Instead, the
metal nucleates as isolated islands that then coalesce as the film grows. For
sub-$10$\,nm Ni films, some residual island-like morphology is likely, reducing
the lateral electrical connectivity and the in-plane metallic character.

\paragraph{Interfacial interdiffusion.}
At the Ni/TiO$_2$ interface, some atomic mixing occurs during deposition at
room temperature. This creates a transition layer with intermediate properties
rather than a sharp interface, which affects both $\epar$ and $\eperp$.

\begin{warnbox}
\textbf{Consequence for the EMT prediction.} All four effects push the
fabricated Ni toward less metallic optical constants relative to Johnson and
Christy's bulk data. A Bruggeman effective medium fit to our experimental data
(discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:forward_validation}) gives an effective metallic
filling factor of $f \approx 0.82$, meaning the deposited Ni behaves as though
only $82\%$ of it has bulk metallic character. The remaining $18\%$ is
optically equivalent to a dielectric inclusion, consistent with the estimated
oxide content and islanding effects described above. This sub-metallic character
shifts the ENZ crossing from the EMT-predicted $607$\,nm toward shorter
wavelengths, and the experimental extraction in Chapter~\ref{ch:results} shows
that $\Re(\epar)$ actually remains positive throughout $550$--$1000$\,nm for
this sample.
\end{warnbox}

\paragraph{Validity of the effective medium approximation.}
Even setting aside thin-film effects on the constants themselves, the EMT
formulas are an approximation. They are derived from the electrostatic
limit $k_0 d \to 0$, where $d$ is the period of the multilayer. For our
stack with period approximately $15$\,nm and the shortest wavelength $550$\,nm:
\begin{equation}
  k_0 d = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\,d \approx \frac{2\pi}{550}\times 15
  \approx 0.17\;\text{rad} \approx 10^\circ.
  \label{eq:ch3_kd}
\end{equation}
This is small enough that the EMT should be a good first approximation, with
corrections of order $(k_0 d)^2 \approx 3\%$. The EMT is not the bottleneck
for accuracy in this system: the uncertainty in the thin-film Ni constants
dominates by a much larger margin.

\section{The Role of EMT in This Report}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Despite its limitations, the EMT prediction plays an essential role in the
analysis. It provides:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{A warm-start for the inverse extraction.} The
  optimisation in Chapter~\ref{ch:inverse_methodology} is initialised with
  the EMT values $(\epar^\text{EMT}(\lambda), \eperp^\text{EMT}(\lambda))$
  at each wavelength, ensuring that the nonlinear solver starts in the correct
  region of parameter space.

\item \textbf{A reference for comparison.} The extracted permittivities in
  Chapter~\ref{ch:results} are plotted alongside the EMT prediction, making
  the deviation from bulk optical constants immediately visible.

\item \textbf{A forward model for IFC classification.} The IFC type
  predicted by EMT (nominally Type-II above $607$\,nm) provides the physical
  context for interpreting the experimental results.
\end{enumerate}

The EMT is a prediction, not a measurement. The goal of the rest of this
report is to replace it with a measurement-based extraction of $\epar(\lambda)$
and $\eperp(\lambda)$ from the actual fabricated sample.

\begin{resultbox}
\textbf{Summary of Chapter~\ref{ch:emt}.} Starting from the electromagnetic
boundary conditions at planar interfaces, we derived that the in-plane
permittivity $\epar = \rho\eps_\text{Ni} + (1-\rho)\eps_\text{TiO_2}$ is the
arithmetic mean (capacitors in parallel) and the out-of-plane permittivity
$\eperp = \eps_\text{Ni}\eps_\text{TiO_2}/(\rho\eps_\text{TiO_2} +
(1-\rho)\eps_\text{Ni})$ is the harmonic mean (capacitors in series). With
$\rho = 0.379$, the EMT predicts an ENZ crossing in $\epar$ at $607$\,nm and
a loss tangent of $2$ to $8$ throughout the visible range. The out-of-plane
permittivity $\eperp$ is dielectric throughout, driven by TiO$_2$. However,
thin-film effects, surface oxidation, and PVD islanding make the fabricated
Ni less metallic than bulk, and the experimental extraction in
Chapter~\ref{ch:results} shows both $\Re(\epar)$ and $\Re(\eperp)$ positive
throughout $550$--$1000$\,nm.
\end{resultbox}
